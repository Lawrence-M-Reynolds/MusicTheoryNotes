A tendency tone is a role that note takes in voice leading when it resolves chromatically (by a semitone) to a structurally stable target tone. A stable tone typically being one of those belonging to a major or minor triad, particularly the tonic or the fifth. The third is still common but is the least stable of the three, and it gives a feeling of continuation.
## Types
### Leading tones
This is a tendency tone that has a pull upwards. For example, the B in the B-F tritone wants to resolve upwards to C.

For example, the 3rd in the dominant V chord (G - M3 - B - m3 - D - m3 - F).
### Upper Leading tone
This is a tendency tone that wants to resolve downwards. So, this will be the F in a tritone in the key of C major.

For example, the 7th in the dominant V chord (G - M3 - B - m3 - D - m3 - F).
## Resolution of dissonant tones
From the above you can see that the general rule of "resolving suspensions downwards" does not always apply.
### Suspensions
This is where a tone from one harmony is held over to another where it becomes dissonant. But in this case the tone resolves downwards. 
### Retardations
This is where a tone from one harmony is held over to another where it becomes dissonant. But in this case the tone resolves upwards.
